Deep Neuroevolution: Genetic Algorithms are a Competitive Alternative for Training Deep Neural Networks for Reinforcement Learning — Paper Summary

Ben Phillips
Nov 16, 2018 · 9 min read

In December 2017, Uber AI Labs released five papers, related to the topic of neuroevolution, a practice where deep neural networks are optimised by evolutionary algorithms.

This post is a summary of one those papers called “Deep Neuroevolution: Genetic Algorithms are a Competitive Alternative for Training Deep Neural Networks for Reinforcement Learning”. It is intended for those with some basic familiarity in topics related to machine learning. Concepts such as ‘genetic algorithms’ and ‘gradient descent’ are prerequisite knowledge.

Much of the research in the five Uber AI Labs papers, including the paper I am summarising, builds on research by OpenAI presented in the paper “Evolution Strategies as a Scalable Alternative to Reinforcement Learning”. OpenAI have written a blog post summarising their paper. A shorter summary (written by me) can be found here.

The code used for the experiments in this paper can be found here. In April 2018, the code was optimised to run on a single personal computer. The work to achieve this is described in an Uber AI labs blog post, and the specific code can be found here.

What were the results?

Deep Neural Networks (DNN) are typically optimised using gradient based methods such a back-propagation. This paper shows that using a simple Genetic Algorithm (GA) it is possible to optimise DNNs and that GAs are a competitive alternative to gradient based methods when applied to Reinforcement Learning (RL) tasks such as learning to play Atari games, simulated humanoid locomotion or deceptive maze problems.

The scalability of the GA was demonstrated by evolving a DNN with over four million parameters, the largest network ever evolved with an evolutionary algorithm. The ability to parallelise means that the computation for GAs can be distributed across many CPUs, creating the potential to train DNNs much more quickly compared to gradient based methods. Additionally, by ingeniously representing the DNN parameters as a series of random seeds, a network with over 4 million parameters can be encoded in just a few thousand bytes (a factor of 10,000-fold smaller).

Novelty Search (NS), a technique used with GAs to encourage exploration in tasks with deceptive or sparse rewards, was shown in one problem domain (called ‘Image Hard Maze’) to outperform other gradient and evolutionary based algorithms that optimise solely for reward.

Amazingly, Random Search (RS) was found to be competitive (although never as competitive as GAs) in learning to play some Atari games. This suggests that in some domains, sampling around the origin is enough to find an adequate solution.

These results indicate that GAs (and RS) are not all out better or worse than other methods of optimising DNN, but that they are a ‘competitive alternative’ that one can add to their RL tool belt.

Like OpenAI, they state that although DNNs don’t struggle with local optima in supervised learning, they can still get into trouble in RL tasks due to a deceptive or sparse reward signal. It is for this reason that non gradient based methods such as GAs can perform well compared to other popular algorithms in RL.

About the GA

For an introduction to GAs, I recommend Vijini Mallawaarachchi’s blog post.

Popular algorithms in RL such as Q-learning and policy gradients use gradient descent. ES also follows the gradient via an operation similar to finite differences. Unlike these methods, GAs do not follow the gradient, instead the use exploration through mutation and exploitation through selection to evolve the individuals of a population.

This work uses a very simple GA, with the purpose being to establish a baseline for EAs. It’s so simple that it doesn’t even use cross-over, a technique so common in GAs that at first it felt strange to even call this algorithm a GA.

After initialising the population, the top T individuals (in this case neural network parameter vectors) are selected to be potential parents. These individuals are then selected from uniformly at random (with replacement) and are mutated by adding Gaussian noise with a standard deviation determined empirically for each experiment. The highest scoring individual of each generation is also added to the next generation (elitism of one).

State-of-the-art Encoding

We propose a novel method to store large parameter vectors compactly by representing each parameter vector as an initialization seed plus the list of random seeds that produce the series of mutations applied to theta. This innovation was essential to enabling GAs to work at the scale of deep neural networks, and we thus call it a Deep GA. This technique also has the benefit of offering a state-of-the-art compression method.

Without crossover, the list of seeds that define an individual is equal to the number of generations. It has the benefit of reduced memory and network transmission costs. With crossover, a new more complex method would be needed to encode the individuals. This method of encoding is pretty cool. Representing millions of neural network parameters with a comparatively tiny number of seeds.

Novelty Search

[novelty search] was designed for deceptive domains in which reward-based optimization mechanisms converge to local optima. NS avoids these local optima by ignoring the reward function during evolution and instead rewarding agents for performing behaviors that have never been performed before (i.e. that are novel).

NS requires a function that can quantify the difference between behavior of any two individuals. After each generation, individuals have a small probability of being added to an archive. The ‘novel’-ness of new individuals are determined by comparing them to others in the same generation as well as those stored in the archive.

Experiments

The experiments focus on the performance of the GA in the following tasks; Learning to play Atari games from pixels, continuous control of a simulated humanoid learning to walk (MuJoCo) and a problem with known deceptive local optima called the ‘Image Hard Maze’.

Atari

13 Atari games were selected. Five were selected because ES performed well on them. Four others were selected because ES performed poorly. Lastly, four that where selected alphabetically from the collection of available games.

The GAs performance on Atari games is compared to the performance of other reinforcement learning algorithms such as DQN (Q-learning), ES and A3C (policy gradients). The results for ES and A3C were both from OpenAI’s paper. To facilitate comparisons, the data preprocessing and network architecture are the same as that presented in a 2015 paper on DQNs. Many improvements have been made to DQNs since 2015, but the authors argue that it makes sense to compare results to a vanilla DQN as this work uses only a vanilla GA. To also allow comparisons with OpenAI’s recent work on ES, the number of game frames agents experience over the course of a GA run is kept constant (at one billion frames) as it was in the ES experiments. This means that agents will see a varying number of frames (depending on how successful they are), and that the number of generations in a GA run is not fixed.

[This research uses] the larger DQN architecture from Mnih et al. (2015) consisting of 3 convolutional layers with 32, 64, and 64 channels followed by a hidden layer with 512 units. The convolutional layers use 8 x 8, 4 x 4, and 3 x 3 filters with strides of 4, 2, and 1, respectively. All hidden layers were followed by a rectifier nonlinearity (ReLU). The network contains over 4M parameters.

There is some difficulty in comparing the results of the GA to other algorithms. Unlike the GA, ES and DQN which were all trained from a random start, the A3C was trained using a database of human starts to sample from. Additionally, the results for the DQN were obtained using approximately 200 million frames (due to excessive computation time), compared to the 1 billion frames used for ES, RS and the GA.

Overall the GA was able to perform roughly as well as the other algorithms (see Table 1 below). DQN, ES and the GA each produced the best score on three games, while A3C produced the best score on four games.

For some games, GA sometimes outperforms DQN and ES within one or tens of generations. Those solutions found in the first generation are essentially random DNNs because there hasn’t even been one round of selection. So was the GA just doing random search? Here RS is a special case of GA, with the number of generations being equal to one. Looking at the results, GA always outperforms RS. But gosh, RS can beat DQN, A3C and ES on some domains.

[T]he success of RS versus DQN, A3C, and ES suggest that many Atari games that seem hard based on the low performance of leading deep RL algorithms may not be as hard as we think, and instead that these algorithms for some reason are performing extremely poorly on tasks that are actually quite easy.

Humanoid Locomotion

The GA is next tested on a continuous control problem, where a simulated humanoid learns to walk. The software used was MuJoCo (Humanoid-v1 environment in OpendAI Gym).

This problem involves mapping a vector of 376 scalars that describe the state of the humanoid (e.g. its position, velocity, angle) to 17 joint torques. The robot receives a scalar reward that is a combination of four components each timestep. It gets positive reward for standing and its velocity in the positive x direction, and negative reward the more energy it expends and for how hard it impacts the ground. These four terms are summed over every timestep in an episode to calculate the total reward.

The GA requires a lot more computation compared to ES to achieve similar results which is surprising given how well GA performed on the Atari games.

Image Hard Maze

Image Hard Maze is a problem that contains local optima (traps) that deceive agents that greedily get closer to the goal.

Novelty Search doesn’t have this problem because it ignores the reward and encourages the agent to visit new places. As mentioned earlier, NS needs a behavioral difference function to determine ‘novel’-ness. For this problem, that function is the squared Euclidean distance between the final positions.

The neural network sees a birds eye view of the maze, which is 84 by 84 pixel image, and has over 4 million parameters. It controls the navigation of the robot in the maze through a speed and rotation output. The three previous frames are also part of the input to provide temporal information.

Performing novelty search allows the GA to solve this task while the GA and ES that optimise only for reward (smallest distance to the goal) get stuck in local optima. DQN and A2C (the source code for A3C was unavailable) were also unable to sufficiently explore enough to compete with the GA-NS.

Conclusion

The experiments demonstrate that GAs are a competitive alternative in RL to other well known algorithms such as DQN, A3C and ES, sometimes performing better and sometimes worse. This work only uses a simple GA and new results may be discovered by combining GAs with other techniques known to be effective in the neuroevolution community, or by combining GAs with other RL algorithms.

While these results seem promising, it is possible to take a slightly more pessimistic view. In march 2018, Ben Recht co-authored a paper called Simple random search provides a competitive approach to reinforcement learning where his team achieved state-of-the art on the MuJoCo walking task with RS. His conclusions are similar to those presented in this paper regarding these results, that the success of RS in these domains indicates that these problems are not as difficult as previously thought.

Nevertheless, the work in the paper does demonstrate that a simple GA can outperform RS and that there are likely further gains to be made by continuing to research in this direction. It also shows that there is a trade off regarding sample efficiency and compute time among the current popular algorithms. This is something that can be taken into account given your use case. If the cost of a sample is high, a gradient based method might be better. If time is the limiting factor, a GA will get you a solution more quickly. Perhaps in the future we will be able to specify a “speed to sample efficiency” trade off and find a solution using both EAs and gradient based methods.
